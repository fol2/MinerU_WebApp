# What is a research article/Paper?  

• It uses a standard format to clearly communicate information about an experiment  

Eight major sections :  

– Title   
–  Abstract   
– Introduction and backgroundthe WHY   
– Literature Surveythe WHAT   
– Method usedthe HOW   
– Experiments & Results   
– Discussion, conclusion and future work   
– and References  

# Article section boundaries  

• Sometimes there are minor variations, such as: –  a combined sections including  

• Results and Discussion, or introduction & Literature review   
– or an overall General Discussion section includes • multiple experiments presented in one article .  
– A finishing section as the conclusion and future work  

• Sections might vary in different ways, but the main sections are there all the time  

How to read an article  

# How to read an article (1..4)  

•Enough Time to read ; allow yourself reasonably enough time, this takes more time than most students realise  

•Understand ; before you can write about the research, you have to understand it  

– Then you need to answer the following: • Does the author's study make sense to you in lay terms ?• Could you explain the study to your peers?  

•Explain ; when you can clearly explain the study in your own words , then you are ready to write about it  

• Here's how to proceed

# How to read an article (3..4)  

3. Underline/highlight key sentences or write the key point of each paragraph in the margin (these are the building blocks of your summary)  

4. Although the abstract can help you to identify the main points, you cannot rely on it exclusively, because it contains highly condensed information  

5. Read for depth, read interactively  

# How to read an article (4..4)  

6. After you have highlighted the main points, read each section several times  

7. As you read, ask yourself these questions:  

–How does the design of the study address the question posed?   
–What are the controls for each experiment?   
–How convincing are the results ?  
–Are any of the results surprising ?  
–How does this study contribute toward answering the original question?   
–What aspects of the original question remain unanswered ?  

Writing the Summary  

# Writing the Summary (1..6)  

• Like an abstract in a published research article, the purpose of an article summary is to give the reader a brief, structured overview of the study  

• To write a good summary, identify Why, What, How – what relevant information is important and condense that information for your reader  

•The better you understand a subject, the easier it is to explain it thoroughly and briefly  

•Writing  the first draft- STORY! – Use the same order as in the article itself –Adjust the length accordingly depending on the content of the particular article

# Writing the Summary (3..6)  

# ... Writing  the first draft  

•The number of suggested sentences given is only a rough guideline for the relative length of each section.  

• For the first draft, focus on content, not length (it will probably be too long), condense later as needed .  

• Try writing about the hypotheses ,methods and results first, then about the introduction and discussion last.  

• If you have trouble on one section, leave it for a while and try another one.  

# Writing the Summary (4..6)  

... Writing  the first draft  

• Edit for completeness and accuracy  

• Add information for completeness where necessary  

• More commonly, if you understand the article , you will need to cut redundant or less important information  

• Stay focused on the research question, be concise, and avoid generalities  

• The Methods summary is often the most difficult part to edit  

# Writing the Summary (5..6)  

# Finalising your summary  

•Edit for style. Write to an intelligent ,interested ,naive , and slightly lazy audience (e.g., yourself, your classmates).  

•Expect your readers to be interested , but don't make them struggle to understand you.  

•Include all the important details ; don't assume that they are already understood.  

– Eliminate wordiness, including most adverbs ("very", "clearly").  

"The results clearly showed that there was no difference between the groups ” can be shortened to   
"There was no significant difference between the groups ".  

– Use specific, concrete language.  

– Use precise language and cite specific examples to support assertions. – Avoid vague references (e.g. " this illustrates "should be "these result illustrates ").

•Use scientifically accurate language .

•Rely primarily on rephrasing , not direct quotes.  

•Direct quotes are seldom used in scientific writing. Instead, rephrasing what you have read.  

•To give due credit for information that you rephrase, cite the author's last name and the year of the study (Smith, 1982). (e.g. “APA Citations”)  

•Re-read what you have written.  

•Ask others to read it to catch things that you have missed.  

# Summarising, Plagiarism, and Conclusion  

# Summarising and Plagiarism  

• Plagiarism is always a risk when summarising someone else’s work. To avoid it:  

– Take notes in your own words   
– Avoid writing complete sentences when note-taking   
– Summarise points in your own words  

– If you find yourself sticking closely to the original language and making only minor changes to the wording, then you probably don't understand the study (see the university handout, about plagiarism)  

# Conclusion  

# Understand the way of summarising an article has more than one benefit:  

•First ; it will be easy for you to draw comparisons between more than one relevant articles  

•Second ; it is an accumulated knowledge/skills helps to plan your road map toward the progression of your research  

•Third ; if you practice summarising articles, it will be easy for you to write your own publication after knowing the main elements that are used to construct an article  

•Fourth ; Summarising different related articles, group them together, and critically analyse them is the building block of your research literature review
